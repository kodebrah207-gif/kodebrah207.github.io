Who is Kwaku Obeng Debrah?

Kwaku Obeng Debrah, born on Wednesday, November 7, 2007, is a promising 18-year old final year high school student currently pursuing the Cambridge Advanced Level Curriculum. His subjects are Biology, Chemistry, and Mathematics. He is determined, disciplined, ambitious, and a person of great repute among his peers and superiors. His academic achievements and roles in various societies have been a testament to the many hours put into ensuring that success becomes reality.

His life-long mission is to come up with alternative curative treatments for the many health conditions that are caused by genetic factors, particularly sickle cell disease, and also by lifestyle choices, ensuring that these treatments are highly cost-effective, easily accessible, and above all, highly successful in curing or nullifying the effects of these diseases.

Life after Mercer: Back to Ghana

As stated earlier, I plan to return to my homeland Ghana to contribute meaningfully to ground-breaking scientific research after my time at Mercer. By the end of my PhD program, I hope to be competent as a researcher who can work independently, a disciplined laboratory leader, and an expert of the highest order in my field of study. More importantly, I believe that I would have gone far in finding a cure to the sickle cell disease.

I hope to play research roles in institutions such as the West African Genetic Medicine Centre in the University of Ghana, Noguchi Memorial Institute for Medical Research and the Sickle Cell Foundation of Ghana. This will provide me with credible institutional backing as I make contributions to clinical and translational research. Having affiliations with such institutions will be of great benefit to all parties involved as I play active roles in community engagement and educating the general public. Also, working in such institutions will strengthen my foundations in molecular biology, biochemistry, and pharmacology, adding to the skills I would have acquired at Mercer University.

The next course of action is to establish my specific field of interest when it comes to sickle cell research. Many advances have been made so far when it comes to preventing and reducing the effect of sickle cell, such as gene therapy, gene editing, bone marrow transplant, hydroxyurea treatment, switching on of the fetal haemoglobin gene(HbF), among others. However, the reality is that many issues arise with such interventions, including the high cost of treatment and lack of easy access to such treatments, unwanted health complications such as random gene mutations, and lack of many quality heath and research centres dedicated to combatting this disease. I intend to focus my research in research and translational science, which involves practically applying fundamental scientific discoveries made about sickle cell. I intend to use my understanding of molecular biology in applying therapeutic treatments that can reduce, and even nullify, the effects of this disease. This work involves assessing, monitoring, and evaluating the effectiveness, safety, and cost of these treatments among sickle cell patients.

I hope to make further contributions by exploring ways and means within the field of pharmacology that are cost-effective and easily accessible to those in need of treatment, and with a very high proven success rate, and also by researching on the way certain genes influence response to drugs among individuals within the Sub-Sarahan population, coming up with the right dosages and ways to implement optimum dosages, and reconfiguring the use of certain drugs and their effects through drug repurposing. Furthermore, I am determined to improve upon already existing solutions to the frequent crises sickle cell patients go through, as well as the risks they face, such as inflammation and damage to the spleen. With these research advancements and contributions, I believe that significant progress would have been made in reducing the prevalence of sickle cell as continuous and highly valued efforts into coming up with a potential cure continues.

However, achieving all this will definitely not be anything close to easy. Securing adequate funding and lack of suitable modern infrastructure are just a few of the challenges I expect to encounter, and these can be reasons for people to give up on such a mission. Nonetheless, I see these challenges as stepping stones and opportunities to rise above adversity, put renewed faith in God, seek clarity, and act in accordance to what is right in the eyes of both God and humanity. The world needs such minds, and I am sure that is definitely something I will learn at Mercer University.
